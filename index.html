Denmark and Bicycles

The cycling culture in Denmark is almost as old as the bicycle itself. Bikes were first introduced to the country in the 1880s and during the 1920s and 1930s, the bicycle became a symbol of equality and freedom. People of all social classes began biking side by side on their way to work, to a bar, or just the countryside on their days off. Strøget, the main shopping street in Copenhagen, became pedestrian only in 1962 and in the 70s, ‘Car Free Sundays’ were introduced in Copenhagen, and there were protests demanding that all of Copenhagen became car-free.


Follow us on this journey, where you will discover what affects the cycling culture in Copenhagen, and which patterns we can observe among the danish cyclists. No Data, no details

In order to make any observations, we need data - and our main dataset is bicycle data from various measuring points throughout the Copenhagen Metropolitan Area, between the years 2005 – 2014. At each of these measuring points, sensors are counting how many bikes are passing each hour and their direction - and this is the data we are analysing!

Having gathered all the bike data, done the initial exploration and cleaning on the complete data set, we ended up with more than 5000 thousand days of data with hourly countings. To the left, you can see where these countings took place and in which years these location were measured. The years which are not measured for some road are indicated with a missing blue dot. Note that every road is not included in every year! This observation is important to keep in mind when analysing this data. For example, it would not be fair to compare how many people bicycled in 2005 and 2006 by comparing the total count each year, since 2006 measured eight additional roads.

Fun fact about the data

Some data exploration revealed a fun little detail in the data. In every year, all roads had no countings between 02:00 and 03:00 around end of March and end October. Can you think of why this is? The answer is that we transition between summer and winter time at these dates and hours!

When do people bicycle the most?

In this first part, we'll begin by looking into the temporal patterns of the data in order to observe patterns in the data as well as investigate our initial expectations, such as:

  • Weekdays busiest hours, will be 8am and 16am.
  • Weekend nights will be busier than usual.
  • Traffic will be towards the city in the morning.
  • People ride less during the winter.
  • Bicycle users' number has increased over time

Hourly patterns

We began by investigating the hourly patterns. This quickly revealed that on average for all locations, the two most traffic-heavy hours are from 08-09 and from 16-17. In the figure below, we see the average number of bicycles per hour of the day. There are three viewing options, where you by toggling 'Total' can see this pattern.

This was also what you would expect as this fits well with the general patterns in the danish society, where lots of people go to and from work, school institutions, etc. during these hours.


But where is everyone heading to? By selecting only one of the other two categories, you can inspect the cyclists going towards the city center or those going away from it. Here things become more interesting, as we see similar patterns to the total distribution, which was not what we expected! For example, when just looking at a single direction for Dronnings Louises Bro, we see a quite salient pattern when inspecting a single direction. This could imply that the direction (of whether people primarily go towards or away from the city), is very dependent on the specific location.


By moving the slider on the interactive map to the right, we can view the direction of traffic on the bike lanes throughout the day. The color of the bubbles in each location shows whether the dominating direction is towards or away from the center of Copenhagen.

And the size tells "how dominating" either direction is - i.e. the larger the buble, the more the traffick is headed in one direction over the other, whereas small bubles indicate similar amount of traffic in each direction. Note that you can click on any location to show the selected bubble's street name.

From this interactive map it is visible that our new hypothesis from inspecting the plot of Dr. Louises Bro was true. Although the overall traffic around Copenhagen is not dominated by traffic either towards or away from the center - the traffic at each location is typically dominated in each directions at different locations during rush hour around Copenhagen. Moreover, note that if one direction dominated in the morning, typically, the other direction dominated in the afternoon as the bubbles' colors switches!


Weekly patterns

During the week though, the scene changes. By exploring the data we came to an obvious, yet fun fact. As seen bellow, Danes seem to bicycle more on some days compared to others based on the 13 measured roads. As expected during the week the patterns are pretty similar, but on Friday afternoons, things slightly change. From the change in the distribution (see circle in plot), it looks like people either leave work earlier, or they choose to stay for a beer and socialize at the company's Friday bar!



If we for a moment just focus on the nights hours of the week (the red bars in the figure), we can recognize an expected pattern where we see that there in general seem to be more activity on the nights of the weekend. This peaked our curiosity in order to see where people are typically headed during the night hours.

Monthly patterns

Now, let's widen the time-perspective and look at the monthly and seasonal patterns. We want to address the question of whether the month or the different seasons, have an impact on how many people choose to use their bikes. In the next plot, it appears that the weeks in December in general have more passing cyclists than other winter months, except from week 51. But this also makes great sense since it is Christmas week, where it is expected that a lot of people travel for the hollidays, while schools, universities and several companies are closed. This might also explain why we also see a similar decrease in July (weeks 27-29) as these are common weeks for travelling abroad for the summer holidays (in the pre-Covid19 era). To conclude, it seems reasonable to hypothesise that there could be a connection to the weather conditions, which we will investigate in the next section. But before we do that, we want to see how patterns develop over the years.



Yearly patterns

Now that we consider the yearly patterns, we cannot average across every road anymore, as this would be a biased comparison based on which roads are included in the different years. Therefore, we instead inspect each of the 13 roads over the years 10 observed years. As we've seen that both seasonality and weekdays/weekends has an impact on the activity, the comparison should primarily be made between years where most of the 365 days where measured as well as evenly between all four seasons.

In the gallery bellow, we can see two plots side by side for each of the locations.

  • On the left side, the plots show how many days during each year the sensor recorded countings, i.e. the closer to the '365 days' bar, the more comparable. Moreover, the colors indicate distribution between seasons, where these preferably should be equally large.
  • The years with four equally large blocks and close to the '365 days' limit in the left plot, are compareable on the right side plots. On these we see the daily number of cyclists counted that year.
Do you see an increasing trend for the compareable years? For most locations we do. However, there are a few examples which implies the opposite like with "Fredensbro" in 2013. But this drop turned out to be a results of road work. You can find more on this and an in-depth analysis of these patterns in (OUR NOTEBOOK LIIIIINK!!!!).

So the data indicates an increasing number of cyclists on these locations. However, these observations are based only on a few streets and the specified years. Ideally, we would have data from many more roads in order to average across these, and thereby would road work and over/under-represented seasons to a larger extent be averaged out. Although it would be tempting to assume that this reflects the exact increase of the number of people in Copenhagen which have chosen to use their bikes, we need to keep in mind that the population of Copenhagen has also increased over the years, which probably accounts for a portion of the increase.

Super Bike Lanes

For this we looked at Hareskovvej, as it's the only one from the streets that became a super bike lane during the years we have data for. The rest were delivered at a later time, most of them in 2017.

In the figure on the bottom-right, we plot the "fair comparisons" based on equal number of days on the left side and the possibly biased (in both directions) on the right side. Every point is the average number of cyclists in the specified month. Each point on that same month (vertically) refers to the different years. The dots are colored blue if they are prior to April 2013 and red if they are after it.

We can then observe that more bicyclists passed on Hareskovvej at every month and year after the opening of the super bike lane, as every red point is higher up than the blue ones. This implies that the super bike lanes do have an impact on the number of people who choose to use their bicycle!

Influence of Super Bike Lanes

A pessimistic prediction would be that we see an 23% increase in the number of bicyclists on Hareskovvej, after it was introduced as a super bike lane. Note that this "pessimistic" prediction is much higher than the yearly increase in Copenhagen's population since 2005 (≈2%). At the same time, it is also higher than the general increase of cyclists on the streets since 2005 (≈17%). A more accuracte prediction would probably be to compute the increase of the average number of cyclists before and after April 2013 on Hareskovvej which is: ≈1.60968

This shows that from the data, we can observe that 61% more people on average for the selected four months, ride on Hareskovvej after it's upgrade to "super". This fits very well with other analyses done on the specific location. Newer analyses with data up to 2018, show an increase of 68% on weekdays and 108% during the weekends. Furthermore, it is also worth-mentioning that our work suggest that the government's goal of a 25% increase on the number of cyclists on this super bike lane has been reached!
